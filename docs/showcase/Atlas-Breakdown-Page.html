The Approach

Every matte painter knows the wall: project a painting onto geometry and it only holds from the camera that shot it — push in and the hidden edges tear to black. The traditional fix is hand-built projection setups and painted clean plates. I wanted to see how much of that a single still could give up on its own.

Atlas Camera recovers the camera — focal length, tilt, horizon, metric height — from one image using a learned prior, then turns monocular depth into projectable geometry (relief mesh, walls, RANSAC planes). The plate is projected back through the recovered camera in a real-time viewport, so a push or an orbit reads with true parallax. Depth-layer inpainting fills what the foreground hid, so the move holds — no video model in the loop.

It's built as 41 composable ComfyUI nodes (~14K lines of Python, 300+ tests) that wire learned camera calibration, monocular depth, and vision-language scale cues into one graph — and it exports straight to Maya, Nuke, and USD. The matte-painting tradition stays intact; the tool just does the projection maths so the artist keeps their eye on the frame.
